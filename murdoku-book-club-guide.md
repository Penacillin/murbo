# Murdoku: Complete Guide for Players with Visual or Cognitive Impairments

This guide explains the game at **murdoku.com** — what you see on screen, what every element means, every rule, and how to play step by step. It applies to any Murdoku puzzle, regardless of the theme, room names, or character names.

---

## What Is Murdoku?

Murdoku is a puzzle game that combines two ideas:

1. **Murder Mystery** — A murder happened. One of the suspects is the killer. Clues tell you where each person was at the time of the crime.
2. **Sudoku logic** — You place each suspect into exactly one square on a grid. No two suspects can share the same row or the same column.

Your job is to figure out exactly which square every person was in. Once everyone is placed correctly, the murderer is revealed — the one person who was alone in the same room as the victim.

---

## An Important Mindset

**Treat every clue as a rule, not a hint.** Each clue card is an absolute fact. There are no red herrings or misleading clues. If a clue says someone was beside a plant, then wherever you place them, a plant must be immediately next to them — no exceptions.

**Treat the map like a crime scene.** The rooms, furniture, objects, and colored areas are not just decoration. Every object on the board — a chair, a shelf, a carpet, a box, a plant — is there because at least one clue refers to it.

## The Screen Layout

The screen has three main areas:

### Left Panel — Suspects

The left side shows all the suspects, arranged in a grid of cards (two or three per row depending on puzzle size). Each suspect has two cards stacked vertically:

- **Portrait card (top):** A drawn illustration of the character's face, with a colored background unique to that person. Their name is written below the face.
- **Clue card (bottom):** A lighter-colored card showing a sentence that describes where that person was during the murder. The most important words in the clue are shown in **bold**.

At the top of the left panel the label **"Suspects"** appears, with the instruction: *"click to select, hold on grid to place."*

When you click a portrait to select a character, a blue border appears around both their portrait and their clue card.

Some puzzles also show a **"General Clues"** section above the suspect cards — a highlighted box containing one or more clues that apply to the entire puzzle rather than to one specific person. For example: *"There were at least two people in each store."* These general clues are constraints on the solution as a whole and must be satisfied alongside every individual clue.

### Center — The Game Board (the Grid)

The center shows the grid — a rectangular arrangement of rows and columns of cells. Grid sizes vary by puzzle and are **not always square**: a 6-suspect puzzle commonly uses a **6×6 grid** (36 cells), a larger puzzle might use **11×11** (121 cells), and some puzzles use a non-square grid such as **12 columns × 14 rows** (168 cells). The grid dimensions are shown in the puzzle title (e.g. "6×6" or "12×14"). The cells are divided into color-coded rooms separated by thick black walls. Each room is labeled with its name in large text somewhere within it.

### Right Panel — Tools

From top to bottom:

- **X button** — activates the X-mark tool to eliminate cells. You can also **right-click any grid cell directly** to place an X without activating this tool first.
- **Eraser/diamond icon** ("hold to clear all squares") — when active, clicking a cell removes its mark. Click and drag to erase multiple cells at once. **Hold this button down** to clear the entire board at once.
- **UNDO** — reverses your last action.
- **Hint** (lightbulb) — reveals one correct placement if you are stuck.
- **SUBMIT** (grayed out until all suspects are placed) — checks your answer.
- **HOW TO PLAY** — reopens the in-game tutorial.
- **?** — additional help.
- **↗** — share the puzzle.
- **Printer icon** — print the puzzle.
- **🔇** — mute or unmute sound.
- **⚙** — options and settings.

## The Rooms

Every Murdoku puzzle has its grid divided into named rooms. Each puzzle has a different theme and different room names, but the structure is always the same:

- Each room covers a group of connected cells.
- Each room has a **distinct background color or pattern** — this is how you tell rooms apart visually.
- Each room has its name written as large text somewhere inside it.
- **Thick black borders** form the walls between rooms. These walls matter for clues that use the word "beside" (explained in the Rules section).

To learn which cells belong to which room, look at the floor color and the room label. You can also **hover your mouse over any cell** — a small tooltip may appear naming a special object in that cell (like "Carpet" or "Box").

### What objects can appear in rooms?

Different puzzles use different objects. Common ones include:

| Object | What it looks like |
|--------|--------------------|
| **Chair / Armchair** | A wide padded seat, usually white or cream-colored |
| **Shelf / Bookshelf** | A tall dark cabinet shape with visible shelves or book spines |
| **Plant / Shrub** | A green leafy plant in a pot |
| **Carpet** | A cell with a distinct rug texture (often bordered/framed) — hover to confirm: the tooltip says "Carpet" |
| **Box** | A rectangular box shape |
| **Table** | A flat rectangular surface |
| **Cash register** | A dark machine shape, found in store-themed rooms |
| **Garbage can** | A dark cylindrical bin |
| **Statue** | A small figurine or sculpture shape |
| **Boulder** | A large round rock, found in outdoor-themed puzzles |
| **Bear / wildlife** | An animal illustration in outdoor-themed puzzles — treated as an object. Clues reference it like furniture ("beside the bear," "four rows below a bear"). Does not block a row or column. |
| **Other objects** | Varies by puzzle theme |

All of these objects only matter because clues refer to them. If no clue mentions "garbage can," that object is irrelevant. Focus on what the clues actually say. When in doubt, **hover over a cell** to see its tooltip label.

## The Suspects

Each puzzle has a set of suspects — the number varies by puzzle size (typically 6 for a 6×6 grid, up to 12 or more for larger grids). They are all listed in the left panel. One of them is the **victim** — the person who was murdered. The victim's clue card is labeled **"The Victim"** at the start of the clue text.

The victim is not the murderer. You still place the victim on the board like everyone else.

On the grid, when you place the victim, they appear as the letter **V** instead of their name's initial.

Every other suspect appears on the grid as the first letter of their name.

### Character Appearance

Each suspect's **portrait illustration** may show them with specific accessories — for example, wearing a cap, wearing glasses, or carrying an item. These visual details are **clue evidence**, not just decoration.

Some clues reference appearance: *"everyone on the Rocky Trail was wearing glasses"* means a suspect without glasses in their portrait cannot be placed on the Rocky Trail. To apply these clues, look at each portrait carefully and note which suspects wear which items.

When a clue references an accessory (e.g. "cap," "glasses"), mentally note which suspects have that item in their portrait, and which do not — then eliminate accordingly.

## The Rules

### Rule 1: One Person Per Row, One Per Column (the Sudoku Rule)

Each row can contain exactly one suspect. Each column can contain exactly one suspect.

When you lock a suspect into a cell (see How to Play), the game automatically marks every other cell in that same row and column with an **X** — those cells are now impossible for anyone else.

**Example:** If you lock someone in row 3, column 2, then the entire rest of row 3 and the entire rest of column 2 get X marks.

This is the most important rule. Every placement eliminates many other options.

### Rule 2: Every Clue Is a Hard Rule

No clue is approximate or optional. Each clue card describes exactly where that person was. Your final placement must satisfy every clue for every suspect simultaneously.

### Rule 3: "Beside" Means Adjacent in the Same Room

"Beside" means the cell directly to the **left**, **right**, **above**, or **below**. Diagonal cells do not count.

**Critically: walls block "beside."** If there is a thick black wall between two cells, they are NOT beside each other, even though they look adjacent on the grid.

This means "beside a plant" only counts if the plant is in the very next cell AND in the same room.

If you hover over the word **"beside"** in a clue card, a tooltip appears: *"to the left, right, up, or down of something, in the same area."*

### Rule 4: Directional Clues — North, South, East, West

Clues can give a direction from one person or object to another:

- **South of** = directly below, same column, next row down.
- **North of** = directly above, same column, next row up.
- **East of** = directly to the right, same row, next column to the right.
- **West of** = directly to the left, same row, next column to the left.

No diagonals. The default meaning of "south of," "north of," etc. is **one step** in that direction.

Some clues specify an exact distance: **"exactly one column east of [person]"** means the suspect is in the same row as the referenced person, exactly one column to their right. The distance could also be more than one: "two rows south of" means two rows down, same column.

Directional distance clues can also reference **objects** (not just people): **"four rows below a bear"** means the suspect is in a row exactly four rows beneath a bear icon. Scan all the object cells of that type and check which row is exactly N rows away.

If suspect B is "south of" suspect A, B is in the very next row below A in the exact same column.

### Rule 5: "Only Person" Conditions

A clue like "the only person sitting in a chair" has two parts:

1. That suspect must be in a cell that contains a chair.
2. No other suspect on the entire board can be placed in any other chair cell.

So even if there are multiple chair cells on the board, only one person can occupy any of them.

Some puzzles extend this to a **category of rooms**: "the only person sitting in any Store" means that across every room labeled as a store, only this one suspect is in a chair. No chair cell in any store room can contain anyone else.

### Rule 6: Room Clues ("in the [Room]")

If a clue says a suspect "was in the [Room Name]," that suspect's cell must be inside that named room. The room name in the clue will match one of the labeled rooms on the board.

Some clues combine a room with an object: **"sitting in a chair in the Walkway"** means the suspect must be in a chair cell AND that cell must be inside the Walkway room. Both conditions must be true simultaneously.

### Rule 7: "Not Beside" Conditions

If a clue says a suspect "was not beside a shelf" (or any object), then none of that suspect's immediately adjacent cells (in the same room) can contain that object.

### Rule 8: "Either...Or" Conditions

Some clues give two possible conditions joined by "or" — for example, "she was either beside a shrub or a plant." This means at least one of those conditions must be true: the suspect must be beside a shrub, beside a plant, or beside both. They cannot be beside neither.

### Rule 9: "Beside the [Room] Area"

A clue can say a suspect was beside a **room** rather than an object — for example, "beside the Fountain area." This means the suspect's cell must be directly adjacent (left, right, above, or below) to at least one cell that belongs to that named room. The suspect does not have to be inside the Fountain room — just touching its border.

### Rule 10: "Corner of a [Room]" Conditions

A **corner cell** is a cell inside a room that is surrounded by walls on two of its four sides — meaning two of its neighboring cells are outside the room (either a different room or outside the board). These are the cells tucked into the inner or outer corners of a room's shape.

A clue like "not in a corner of a Store" means the suspect cannot be in any corner cell of any store-type room. To apply this, look at each store room and identify which cells are corner cells (they have walls or other rooms on two sides), then eliminate those cells for that suspect.

### Rule 11: "Alone With the Murderer" (the Victim's Clue)

The victim's clue always says they were alone with the murderer. This means:

- Only **two** suspects are placed in the victim's room: the victim and the murderer.
- All other suspects are in other rooms.
- After you solve the board, whichever suspect shares the victim's room is the murderer.

## How to Play — Step by Step

### Step 1: Read All the Clues Before Touching Anything

Read every clue card before placing anyone. Understanding all clues together is essential — they constrain each other. A single clue in isolation might allow many options; combining all clues usually reduces each suspect to one or two possible cells.

### Step 2: Identify the Objects on the Board

Before solving, hover your mouse over cells that appear to have objects (furniture, plants, etc.) to read their tooltips. Know where every chair, shelf, plant, carpet, and other object is located. Cross-reference with the clues to know which objects matter.

### Step 3: Select a Suspect

Click the **portrait card** of the suspect you want to place. A blue outline appears around their cards. The instruction bar at the bottom of the screen reminds you: *"Tap the grid to write a note, or press and hold to place this character."*

### Step 4a: Place and Lock a Suspect (Confirmed Placement)

With a suspect selected, move to a grid cell and **hold the mouse button down** for about one second. A **circle animation** appears while you hold. When the animation completes, the suspect is locked into that cell.

When locked:
- Their letter (or **V** for the victim) appears large and bold in the cell.
- The entire row and entire column get **X marks** — no one else can go there.
- A message confirms the row and column are now blocked.

You can also **left-click** a cell to place the suspect without the hold animation — this places them immediately. To **remove** a placed suspect, click their cell again.

### Step 4b: Write a Pencil Note (Tentative Mark)

A quick single click on a cell (while a suspect is selected) places a small letter in the corner — a **pencil note**. This marks a cell as a possibility without committing to it.

Pencil notes do **not** trigger X marks in the row and column. They do not count as placements for the purpose of submitting.

To clear a pencil note, click the cell again or press Undo.

### Step 5: Mark Impossible Cells with X

Use the **X tool** (right panel) or **right-click** any cell to mark it as impossible. This is how you record eliminations based on your reasoning — cells where you know a suspect cannot go.

You can **click and drag** with the X tool active to mark multiple cells at once.

### Step 6: Remove Marks with the Eraser

Click the **eraser button** (right panel) to activate it, then click or drag across cells to clear their marks. To reset the entire board, **press and hold** the eraser button until it clears everything.

### Step 7: Submit Your Answer

Once every suspect is placed and locked (large letters visible, not pencil notes), the **SUBMIT** button becomes available. Click it to check your answer.

- **If correct:** The murderer is revealed and the puzzle is solved.
- **If incorrect:** The game tells you **how many placements are correct**, so you can identify and fix the wrong ones.

## Solving Strategy

These principles apply to any Murdoku puzzle:

**1. Start with the most restricted suspects.**
Find the clues that allow the fewest possible cells. A clue like "in the Bedroom" limits the suspect to only the cells inside one room. A clue like "on a carpet" limits the suspect to only the carpet cells — often just one or two on the board. Start there.

**2. Work object-first, not suspect-first.**
Before asking "where can suspect X go?", ask "where are the chairs?" and "where are the carpets?" Find all the objects on the board, then cross-reference with the clues. This is often faster.

**3. Use the Sudoku rule aggressively.**
Every time you lock someone, eliminate that row and column from consideration for everyone else. After placing two or three suspects, the grid often narrows dramatically.

**4. Use paired clues.**
If one suspect's clue is "south of [another suspect]," those two are linked — they share a column and are in adjacent rows. Narrow down one and you narrow down the other.

**5. Use negative clues to eliminate cells.**
"Not beside a shelf" tells you which Library cells are forbidden for that suspect. Mark those with X. This is often faster than looking for valid cells directly.

**6. Use the victim's clue last.**
After placing everyone else, only one room will have exactly one other suspect in it — that room is where the victim goes. The victim's "alone with the murderer" clue becomes the final confirmation.

**7. Hover constantly.**
Hover over clue words to get definitions. Hover over grid cells to identify objects. The game gives you labels on demand — use them.

## Glossary of Key Terms

These are the words that appear in clues and what they mean precisely:

| Term | Exact Meaning |
|------|--------------|
| **beside [object]** | Directly adjacent — one cell to the left, right, above, or below — AND in the same room (not across a wall). Diagonals do not count. |
| **beside the [Room] area** | The suspect's cell is directly adjacent to at least one cell belonging to that named room. The suspect does not need to be inside that room. |
| **south of** | In the cell directly below, in the same column. |
| **north of** | In the cell directly above, in the same column. |
| **east of** | In the cell directly to the right, in the same row. |
| **west of** | In the cell directly to the left, in the same row. |
| **exactly N columns east/west of [person]** | In the same row as that person, exactly N columns to their right (east) or left (west). |
| **only person [condition]** | No other suspect on the board can satisfy the same condition. |
| **only person in any [category]** | Across all rooms of that type (e.g. all stores), only this suspect satisfies the condition. |
| **alone with** | Only the victim and the murderer are placed in that room. No one else. |
| **in the [Room]** | The suspect's cell is inside the named room (same floor color, same labeled area). |
| **in the [Room], beside the [Room] area** | Two simultaneous constraints: the suspect must be inside the first room AND adjacent to the second room. |
| **not beside [object]** | None of the suspect's immediately adjacent cells (same room) contain that object. |
| **either beside X or Y** | At least one of the two conditions must be true: beside X, beside Y, or beside both. |
| **corner of a [Room]** | A cell inside the room that has walls or other rooms on two of its four sides — the geometrically "cornered" cells at the edges of a room's shape. |
| **not in a corner of a [Room/category]** | The suspect cannot be in any corner cell of that room or category of rooms. |
| **N rows below/above a [object]** | The suspect is in a row exactly N rows beneath or above a specific object (e.g. a bear). Find all cells of that object type and calculate which row is N away. |
| **[accessory] clues (cap, glasses, etc.)** | Some clues restrict room placement based on what a suspect wears in their portrait. Inspect portraits carefully — if a clue says "everyone here wore glasses," only suspects shown with glasses in their portrait can go there. |
| **wildlife / bear** | An animal icon on the grid — treated as an object. Referenced in clues like furniture. Does not block a row or column. |
